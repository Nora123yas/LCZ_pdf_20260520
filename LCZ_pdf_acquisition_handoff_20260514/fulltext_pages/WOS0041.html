Abstract

Local climate zone (LCZ) map, an outcome of a supervised classification procedure using satellite imagery, can be generated at different landscape resolutions. Because of the large spatial extent, huge-sized satellite imagery, and fine granularity, it is difficult to analyze supervised LCZ (pixel-classified satellite image) outcome creating a scope for some post-classification tasks. In this paper, we have proposed an entropy-based directional edge algorithm for locating LCZ boundaries, named as DEALB, which creates homogeneous LCZ regions and delineates their boundaries. In DEALB, an image is initially partitioned into superpixels using directional edges considered at different angles (0°, 90°, 45°, and 135°) within a specified spatial scale. Next, similar but spatially cohesive superpixels are clustered to form large homogeneous regions. Spatial cohesiveness, which is a crucial characteristic to be considered in landscape clustering, is implemented by using the breadth-first search and deque data structure. Further, to validate the correctness and pureness of boundaries in the absence of any ground truth image, we have proposed the concept of boundary purity index focusing on spatial contrast inside and outside of LCZ regions. We have demonstrated the algorithm on LCZ classified results for heterogeneous landscape of the city Nagpur in India that has been found useful by domain experts.

Introduction

A Local climate zone (LCZ) is a culturally neutral and climate-oriented landscape classification scheme concentrating on urban forms and functions (Stewart & Oke, 2012). As per the LCZ concept, any urban area (city) can be segmented into seventeen zones (10 Urban + 7 Natural). Studies on LCZ include mapping LCZs from satellite imagery using various machine learning and deep learning models (Bechtel & Daneke, 2012; Kim et al., 2011; Qiu et al., 2018; Tankoyeu et al., 2017; Yoo et al., 2019), analysing LCZs using land surface temperature (LST) (Bartesaghi-Koc et al., 2018; Cai et al., 2018; Geletič et al., 2016; Zhao, 2018), understanding subjective thermal perceptions (Lau et al., 2019) and evaluating heat stress (Verdonck et al., 2018) etc. Though the LCZ concept has become a standard for various urban domains, the task of LCZ mapping still remains interesting for research due to heterogeneous urban morphologies. Till date, many LCZ mapping methodologies using geographical information system (GIS) (Failed, 2018; Geletič et al., 2016; Hammerberg et al., 2018; Unger et al., 2014) and remote sensing (RS) (Bechtel et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2011; Liu & Shi, 2020; Qiu et al., 2018; Yoo et al., 2019) datasets are developed. Generally, GIS-based LCZ outcome is more accurate (Geletič et al., 2016; Unger et al., 2014) than RS-based, but it requires a large quantity of data which is unavailable for numerous cities (Brousse et al., 2016). Due to the consistent availability of remote sensing datasets (Landsat, Sentinel, etc.) in different cities, they are highly preferred for LCZ mapping task. World urban database and access portal tools (WUDAPT) provide a widely accepted protocol for LCZ mapping (Aslam & Irfan, 2022). It has been tested for more than 370 cities spread over different continents (Sigler et al., 2022). The WUDAPT protocol makes use of a freely available Landsat dataset with a random forest classifier implemented in SAGA GIS software. To efficiently identify LCZs in any urban region, uniformity in urban form (uniform characteristics of urban morphology at the local spatial scale) is the key. Uniformly structured cities depict distinct categorization of LCZs (Bechtel & Daneke, 2012; Unger et al., 2014) whereas variations in LCZs were observed for heterogeneously structured dense and compact cities (Cai et al., 2016; Kotharkar & Bagade, 2018; Ren et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2017). The LCZ scheme helps to visualize and identify the heterogeneity in spatial variations at the local scale.

The standard protocol implemented by WUDAPT and many LCZ mapping techniques in the literature utilizes pixel-based supervised classification. In the pixel-based supervised LCZ classification approach, an LCZ label is assigned to every pixel in satellite imagery based on spectral characteristics. As a single pixel portrays a small part of the object of interest and does not constitute an LCZ class, a post-classification method becomes necessary to achieve fine-scale granularity (Demuzere et al., 2021) and cohesive LCZ regions. Also, validation of pixel-based LCZ outcome by an urban expert becomes difficult due to huge-sized satellite imagery and large spatial extent. To overcome this issue, a high-level drawing of distinct LCZs is essential, which allows urban planners to analyze the result, validate the correctness, visualize heterogeneity in urban form and suggest improvements. There are some post-classification strategies in the literature with respect to LCZ, such as the majority filter (Demuzere et al., 2020) and Gaussian filter (Demuzere et al., 2021). The majority filter, with a 300 m radius as the default parameter, was proposed by traditional WUDAPT, but it has some limitations: first, it gives equal importance to the center pixel and the pixel on the border of the mask; second, it tends to withdraw the linear features like a river. The morphological Gaussian filter overcomes these limitations of majority filter by incorporating a likelihood map per class with parameters like standard deviation (σ) and kernel size (> = 2 σ) (Demuzere et al., 2021). It demands an expert’s decision to decide on these values and prior knowledge of the procedure. Though these methods try to smoothen the classification result and achieve fine-scale granularity at some level, the LCZ outcome still remains pixel-based. A next-level LCZ outcome demands the grouping of identical and spatially cohesive pixels to form clusters as many urban planning decisions requires LCZ result as a representation of LCZ regions. A clustered LCZ outcome is necessary because most urban planning decisions require a bigger scale. According to urban experts, clustered LCZ map results can be considered as better output for further urban planning decisions as it reduces the granularity to large homogeneous regions. Although, clustering helps to form homogeneous LCZ regions but still some problems need localization of LCZ boundaries. These problems are summarized as:

  1. (1)

    The LCZ scheme allows forming heterogeneous LCZs (i.e. one LCZ is embedded in other). In the absence of any standard protocol the relevant questions are; how the class embeddings can be figured out? How to decide the landscape architecture plan for the specified LCZ region?

  2. (2)

    How to study the effect of spatial pattern near the LCZ boundary on inter-LCZ and intra-LCZ (neighbouring LCZ) parameters such as temperature variations, humidity variations, electricity consumption etc.

To some extent, identifying LCZ regions and localizing their boundaries enables to solve such regionalized issues. Hence, our aim is to propose a workflow for creating homogeneous LCZ regions as well as to identify their boundaries as a post-classification approach. In this paper, we have presented the underlying framework using a regionalization task (partitioning geographical landscapes into homogeneous and spatially continuous units (Openshaw, 1973) incorporating a categorical raster dataset. Earlier the categorical raster was used in the regionalization concept because it is easy and computationally efficient to work on categorical rasters (Jasiewicz et al., 2018; Stepinski et al., 2015). In addition, our aim is to propose a boundary purity index as a measure of boundary purity in the absence of any ground truth image. The workflow presented in this paper reduces the original spatial data into coarser data units which is generally recommended for scientific query. Such high level representation of landscapes provides good insight for spatial analysis in the fields such as climatology, urban and regional planning, landscape ecology, public policy (Cheruvelil et al., 2013; George et al., 1997; Long et al., 2010).

The work presented in this paper is focused on LCZ maps (pixel-classified Landsat eight image). However, it does not imply that it is useful only for such data. The key idea is to design a methodology which is able to distinguish between different local patterns of cell values in raster, and group them into regions of similar patterns with location of their boundaries. To achieve the mentioned goal, we have proposed an algorithm named as DEALB (directional edge algorithm for locating border). The foundation of this algorithm lies in the regionalization and clustering concepts. Initially, the categorical raster is transformed into superpixels. A superpixel represents a batch of pixels in the image that share similar characteristics. Next, we have identified the boundary of the LCZ region by clustering spatially connected but similar superpixels. Further, we proposed a boundary purity index based on spatial contrast inside and outside the LCZ region, to analyze the correctness or purity of the border drawn. The contributions of the paper are as follows:

  • We have proposed a post-classification workflow to create homogeneous LCZ regions and identify their boundaries generating high level LCZ outcome beneficial for further analysis. This workflow incorporates the formation and clustering of superpixels. Superpixels are formed by incorporating directional edges at different angles (90, 0, 45 and 135 degrees) within a spatial unit of size 300 m. Further, superpixels are merged to form coarser units (polygons) by using clustering algorithm. Clustering algorithm makes use of Deque and breadth first search technique to maintain spatial cohesion among superpixels.

  • We have proposed two boundary purity indices (BPPI-boundary pixel based purity index and BRPI-boundary region based purity index) to analyse the spatial contrast inside and outside of the boundary drawn between the regions. They are proposed to prove the correctness of the border without any ground-truth image.

The organisation of this paper is as follows: The related work and overview of regionalization methodologies is detailed in Sect. "Related work on regionalisation". Sect. "Material and methods" presents input data and introduces methodology, followed by results and an overview about comparable methodologies in literature in Sect. "Results" and "Comparison with other methodologies" respectively. Discussion on the feasibility and comparison of the proposed methodology over traditional regionalization is given in Sect. "Discussion". Sect. "Novelty" mentions about the novelty and further scope of the work. Finally, the work is summarised and concluded in Sect. "Conclusion".

Related Work on Regionalisation

Regionalization is a special clustering technique in which the elements are grouped based on two conditions—(a) similarity and (b) spatial connectivity(Aydin et al., 2021) (Carvalho et al., 2009), resulting in generalised landscape map facilitating useful insights across a large area. In geographical information science, identifying sub-regions within the study area using unsupervised learning methods (DBSCAN, Hierarchical clustering, K-means clustering etc.) is popular (Aydin et al., 2021) (Yuan et al., 2015) (Duque et al., 2007). Clustering algorithms are advantageous over search-based heuristic methods such as AZP (automated zoning procedure) as they are the greedy algorithms that embed the optimization function (Stepinski et al., 2015). However these algorithms require explicit information about geo-locations. In general, regionalization methodologies are categorised into two groups- (1) spatially implicit and (2) spatially explicit regionalization (Duque et al., 2007). Spatially implicit regionalization methods create the homogeneous regions but spatial continuity is not always guaranteed and it requires post-processing step to impose spatial connectivity with the help of subject-matter expert’s knowledge (Openshaw, 1973). In contrast, explicit regionalisation ensures spatial connectivity within the resultant regions by imposing spatial constraints in the clustering algorithms (Duque et al., 2007). Some examples of such methods are self organising maps (Bação et al., 2004), constrained version of k-means (Wagstaff et al., 2001), agglomerative hierarchical clustering (Failed, 2005). There exists some literature focussing on extending spectral clustering with spatial constraints for regionalization (Yuan et al., 2015) (Failed, 2003) (Kawale & Boley, 2013). The spatial constraints such as ML (must link) and CL (can not link) were used to define the affinity matrix of data in spectral clustering (Failed, 2003). Another group of explicit regionalization methods that deals with segmentation concept also creates partitions for spatial data, mainly focussing on raster type dataset (Felzenszwalb, 2004) (Shih & Cheng, 2005). These methods arrange the rasters in a regular lattice.

Number of regions to be created is the most crucial parameter with respect to regionalization methodologies. For some spatially explicit regionalization techniques, number of regions is an endogenous parameter. Such techniques aim to find homogeneous regions without explicitly defining the target number of regions (Aydin et al., 2021). Among the spatially explicit methods where the target number of regions is an explicit parameter, exact regionalization method provides an optimal solution. However, exact methods are computationally expensive and hence they are efficient only for a small number of regions (Duque, 2004). Though there exist number of methods for spatial regionalization, but the use of one or another method is not an arbitrary decision. For the regions where the shape is guided by spatial distribution of variables, conventional clustering with < x, y > co-ordinates will not be appropriate as it tends to generate the circular regions (Duque et al., 2012). All the existing traditional methods give emphasis on constraints like homogeneity and spatial contiguity, disregarding identification and localisation of region boundaries. Identifying and locating region boundaries becomes important to analyse the change in landscape patterns over time, heterogeneity within the region (i.e. if one landscape pattern is embedded inside other) and scale of heterogeneity. The method proposed in this paper ensures homogeneous regions by employing Shannon’s entropy formulation and imposes the continuity constraints with the help of neighbourhood information without explicitly defining the target number of regions on the categorical raster.

Material and Methods

This section gives details about the input dataset and the proposed algorithm (DEALB) for identifying LCZ boundaries. We used the LCZ classified results of the Nagpur region in India for the underlying framework.

LCZ Raster Input Data

For the input to regionalization and boundary localizing algorithm we used pixel-classified Landsat 8 raster (Vaidya et al., 2024). It is a LCZ multi-categorical raster covering the Nagpur region in India with a spatial resolution of 30 m resulting in 446,224 cells. LCZ raster is a result of a random forest classification of Landsat-8 satellite images into distinct LCZ categories. This random forest LCZ classification framework considers the spectral, textural and contextual features embedded in Landsat imagery for identifying the LCZ for each pixel in a heterogeneous urban environment. The classification accuracy statistics reported in Vaidya et al. (2024) for some randomly selected pixels in considered Landsat 8 image for each LCZ type is tabulated in Table 1. In a heterogeneous urban environment, the possibility of existence of mixed LCZ categories at local spatial scale (characteristics of more than one LCZ) increases. The LCZ classification result for the Nagpur is highly heterogeneous in nature ((Kotharkar & Bagade, 2018; Vaidya et al., 2024)) and in such environment it becomes difficult to identify class embeddings and heterogeneity scale as mentioned in the earlier section. Hence, to understand and analyse a pixel-based LCZ outcome better in terms of heterogeneity, we have proposed a post-classification strategy to localize the LCZ region boundaries in this paper.

DEALB (Directional Edge Algorithm for Locating Border)

Figure 1 illustrates the concept of DEALB applied on a test site of 30 m resolution LCZ raster. The proposed DEALB methodology is made up of three main processing modules: (1) superpixel formation—deals with creating homogeneous partitions covering the entire image (2) clustering—merging neighbouring superpixels and (3) Employing the proposed boundary purity index (BPI)—to validate the purity of boundary drawn. These steps are detailed as follows.

Superpixel Formation

Superpixel, a term in computer vision and image processing, represents a group of pixels in the image that share similar features. The motivation for forming superpixels is to capture meaningful regions in an image, as regions provide information about the structure of the scene, thereby making other processing tasks simpler (rather than using a single pixel) (Nixon & Aguado, 2019). Techniques to create superpixels are broadly divided into two categories (1) graph-based and (2) clustering techniques (Nixon & Aguado, 2019). The first method considers an image as a graph where each pixel is termed as a node and edges are formed between pairs of nodes. Each edge has a weight which is used as a similarity measure between pixel pairs. Disjoint regions are obtained by removing edges such that only pixels within the region are interconnected. Second method, clustering (also called as region growing), employs the proximity of pixels in an image for region formation. The redundancy between the pixels of the image is reduced by using superpixels thereby reducing the post-processing difficulty (Chen et al., 2020). As far as the landscape studies are concerned, geographical continuity, intra-region homogeneity and inter-region heterogeneity are the vital characteristics to follow in the superpixel technique. Landscape patterns are generally observed in irregular shapes; hence superpixel technique proposed in this paper follows the directional edge drawing mechanism in a specified dimension of size ‘w’. Initially, a classified raster image is divided into square sized block, referred as grids, in a computationally effective time. Grid is the smallest processing element with \(w\times w\) cells representing the pattern of the categorical classes. Spatial scale represented by the grid is set to 300 m for the underlying domain. The spatial scale selection is the crucial step as landscape patterns are highly dependent on it. Each grid has a single attribute- LCZ feature calculated from the pixels contribution within the grid by using Shannon’s entropy (Shannon, 1948) value from information theory.

Shannon’s entropy is the feasible way to describe landscape patterns (Wang & Zhao, 2018) and is calculated using Eq. (1).

$$E\left(g\right)=-\sum_{i=0}^{n}\, {{p}_{i}\,log\,p}_{i}$$
(1)

where, E(g)- entropy of grid ‘g’ under consideration, pi – probability of ith LCZ in grid ‘g’ and n—number of unique LCZ patterns observed in a grid. Our objective is to form homogeneous regions; E(g) determines the homogeneity value of the grid. Homogeneity of the grid refers to the stationarity of the pattern within it. LCZ feature, a label to be assigned to the grid, is decided by mapping its entropy value with entropy threshold ‘T’. The entropy threshold is incorporated to avoid the effect of spatially dispersed but similar pixels. For example, Fig. 2a shows the simple grid with three types of LCZ categories, 1, 2 and 3. The pixels contributions of these LCZ classes are 85%, 8%, and 7%, respectively. The Shannon’s entropy value for the grid (as shown in Fig. 2a) is 0.9. Though the pixels of LCZ 2 and 3 contribute to the entropy value 0.29 and 0.26, but they are not able to form regions due to spatial discontinuity. The proportion of such spatially dispersed pixels in a 300 m grid is observed to have been 8–10% for the study area. Hence, the entropy threshold ‘T’ of 10% is selected for homogeneity test. If the entropy value of the grid is less than or equal to threshold ‘T’, the grid is termed as homogeneous superpixel. A superpixel list is maintained which acts as a repository for all homogeneous superpixels. Special signature < superpixel co-ordinates (boundary co-ordinates), LCZ > is used to represent a superpixel. The grids with spatial contrast more than ‘T’ are referred as heterogeneous grids. A 300 m heterogeneous grid (for region of interest) is found have pixel contributions for at maximum four different LCZs as shown in Fig. 2b. Further, a heterogeneous grid is segmented to achieve homogeneous superpixels.

All heterogeneous grids are segmented by drawing a directional edge within the grid. A grid is reduced to a spatial unit of a 3X3 window for segmentation purpose. Directional edge can be broadly divided into four types based on the angles at which they are drawn. The angles considered here are 90, 0, 45 and 135, as they guarantee the length of each region formed during the process is as per the minimum length requirement of LCZ. Pixels in a 3X3 window size are represented as in Fig. 3. Segmentation process considers the center pixel ‘P5’ and directional edges are drawn from the center pixel at an angle of 90, 0, 45 and 135 (Fig. 4). Each directional edge is drawn in two directions, forward and backward, dividing the grid into two regions R0 and R1. Regions formed by directional lines and their pixels contribution is tabulated in Table 1. The boundary line to be considered for each region formed is illustrated in Fig. 4.

The edge direction mechanism results in 16 regions (superpixels) and 8 boundaries (see Table 2 and Fig. 4). The regions formed by each directional edge are represented by \({R}_{0}^{s}\) and \({R}_{1}^{t}\) where, ‘s’ and ‘t’ are the sequence numbers given to regions. The next step is to select a feasible superpixel among 16 superpixels and its boundary. Equations 2 and 3 is applied to select feasible boundary and superpixels by considering the homogeneity differences in forward and backward regions represented by \({\text{RE}}_{\text{i}}\) (1 < i < 8).

$${\text{Boundary}}~\left( {\text{b}} \right) = \min \{ {\text{RE}}_{{\text{i}}} \} ~\,{\text{where}}\,~1 \le {\text{i}} \le 8~$$
(2)
$${\text{RE}}{\text{i}}\text{ = mod}\left({\text{e}}_{0}-{\text{e}}_{1}\right){\text{i}}$$
(3)
$$\text{where }{\text{e}}_{0}= \text{ } { R}_{0}^{s}\text{ and }{\text{e}}_{1}= \text{ } {R}_{1}^{t}$$

Boundary (b) is the feasible edge that is considered as boundary between regions formed by any directional edge. RE1, RE2,,……. RE8 represent the homogeneity differences in forward and backward regions formed by edges 1 to 8. Homogeneity difference calculated by Eq. (3) is based on Shannon’s entropy values ‘e0’ and ‘e1’ The value of ‘e0’ is calculated for the region \({R}_{0}^{s}\) and ‘e1’ is calculated for \({R}_{1}^{t}\) where s = 1,3,5,7,9,11,13,15 and t = 2,4,6,8,10,12,14,16 respectively.

Clustering

A traditional approach of image regionalization clusters its smallest elements (pixels) in the data space without regards to spatial contiguity. Clustering in such a way does not guarantee spatial cohesion of elements which is the desired outcome for regionalising landscape patterns. In this paper, we have proposed a feasible approach that includes creating homogeneous partitions covering entire image followed by merging identical neighbours to form coarser polygons. A clustered LCZ output is more suitable for further urban planning decisions as it constitutes large homogeneous regions.

Clustering method (Fig. 5) presented in this paper uses a bottom up and greedy approach. It makes use of techniques such as breadth first search and data structure Deque. Breadth first search is incorporated here to identify neighbours of superpixels. Data structure Deque is used to keep track of similar and dissimilar superpixels. Here, the label (LCZ class) of the superpixel is considered to identify similar and dissimilar superpixels. The basic idea is to consider any superpixel and then to go on tracing it’s all neighbours in the breadth first search manner until any dissimilar superpixel is found. During this process, all the superpixels with similar characteristics are appended to the left end of Deque while dissimilar superpixels are added to the right end. Further, all the similar superpixels are popped from left end of Deque and merged into a single large polygon. While merging the similar superpixel represented as polygons, all the internal and shared polygon edges are removed and only outer edge is retained as a boundary of a polygon.

Boundary Purity Index (BPI)

In this paper, we propose BPI as a measure for validating the correctness of a border between all pair of regions. The term “purity” refers to how well the drawn line separates the different landscapes correctly. In the absence of the standard rule for deciding the location of a boundary, the quality of the drawn boundary is verified by using the corresponding ground truth landscape image. This process is called as supervised evaluation method. We propose the boundary purity index that examines the boundary line without any ground truth image i.e. by using an unsupervised evaluation method. A good boundary is defined by different landscape features on both sides (inner and outer) of it. In other words, intra-region homogeneity and inter-region heterogeneity are the two properties that should be followed by every region boundary. Based on this concept we propose the following mentioned two boundary purity indices here.

Boundary Pixel based Purity Index (BPPI)

A boundary pixel is a pixel with different spatial signatures on either side of it. BPPI considers spatial landscape features contrast along the boundary pixels of each region. It examines the boundary pixel’s neighbourhood, both inside and outside respectively. The difference in the landscape contrast is averaged by the number of boundary pixels. The landscape contrast determines the pixels contribution inside and outside of the border pixel. BPPIR for any region is calculated by using formula (4)

$${BPPI}_{R}= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}{C}_{Ow}^{i}-{C}_{Iw}^{i}$$
(4)

BPPIR is the boundary pixel based purity index for region ‘R’, n is the number of boundary pixels for the region ‘R’, \({C}_{Ow}^{i}\) and \({C}_{Iw}^{i}\) represents outside and inside landscape contrast for the ith boundary pixel over the neighbourhood of size ‘w’ calculated using Shannon’s entropy (Eq. (1)). The value of BPPIR is expected to be as low as possible. The zero value indicates that the boundary correctly separates the regions.

Boundary Region Based Purity Index (BRPI)

Region based purity index uses the fact that landscape characteristics of pixels within the region are similar while they are different for the pixels in the neighbouring region. By following this concept, the proposed region based index validates the region dependency by using the mutual information concept. Mutual information is the term in probability theory which determines the dependence between two random variables by measuring the information shared between them. BRPI is the probabilistic and pair-wise approach of boundary purity. If boundary ‘B’ results in 4 regions RC, R1, R2, R3 and if RC is the center region for which boundary purity is to be found, then the boundary purity for RC is the averaged mutual information score for pairs (RC, R1), (RC, R2) and (RC, R3). The mutual information score between a pair of regions equal to zero means these regions are independent and the boundary is pure. BRPIR for any region is calculated by using formula (5)

$${BRPI}_{R}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}I\left(R,{R}_{i}\right)$$
(5)

BRPIR is the region based purity index for region ‘R’, n is the number of neighbouring regions of ‘R’ and I(R, Ri) is the mutual information score between region ‘R’ and its ith neighbouring region Ri.. In other words, I(R, Ri) is the difference of entropy for region R and conditional entropy of R and Ri.

Algorithms

We have developed three algorithms in locating LCZ boundaries (1) Grid formation (2) Superpixel creation and (3) Clustering of superpixels. Before all the algorithms are executed, a preprocessing step is applied on LCZ classified image. An image considered for LCZ mapping consists of twelve bands representing spectral reflectance values for different ground surface materials. A categorical band representing the LCZ class value for each pixel is formed after the pixel based supervised classification. For boundary localizing purpose, only the categorical values assigned to each pixel in the Landsat 8 image is a priori. Hence, a multispectral LCZ raster is transformed into a data structure LCZ Raster Attribute Table (LRAT). LRAT is a 2D grid in which each cell is represented by tuple representing location and LCZ feature < row, column, LCZ feature > . The algorithms proposed are described next along with their pseudo-codes.

Grid Formation

This algorithm consist of a function grid_creation() which takes the LCZ raster attribute table (LRAT) and grid_size as input and generates the grids of specified size (regular size).

Superpixel Creation

The algorithm described for superpixel creation generates the list of superpixels based on the value of entropy threshold ‘T’ of each grid formed in the grid creation step. The function implemented here is superpixel_creation() which takes list of grids formed in grid_creation function and returns the list of superpixels.

Clustering of Superpixels

The process of clustering generates homogeneous LCZ regions by merging similar and spatially cohesive superpixels. The output of the clustering is named as LCZ map. The LCZ map portrays various LCZs spread over the region of interest with specified color codes. While clustering similar and spatially cohesive superpixels, their internal boundaries are removed retaining only the outer boundary. Such boundary traced LCZ map output is beneficial for various urban planning decisions. Here, we implemented the function clustering_superpixels() which returns the LCZ map representation.

To summarize, grid_creation() is the primary step that transforms a pre-processed LCZ raster into a 2D grid structure of specified spatial resolution. Next, grids with spatial heterogeneity exceeding the threshold are segmented to form superpixels as a result of the execution of the superpixel_creation() algorithm. The outcome of these steps is recorded in a file with signature < superpixel, < boundary co-ordinates, LCZ >  > . Further, the clustering method identifies the spatially cohesive but similar superpixels in a greedy way and merges them to form homogeneous LCZ regions. It dissolves all the shared borders of superpixels retaining only outer boundary. The clustering_superpixels() method utilises breadth first search technique to identify spatially cohesive similar superpixels and a deque data structure is used to keep track of such superpixels.

Results

In this section we demonstrate the results of our algorithm. The algorithm was applied to the two dimensional dataset representing different LCZs for the Nagpur city region in Maharashtra (India). The dataset was represented by a GeoTIFF file having a per pixel resolution of 30 m. Though the overall mechanism of our algorithm seems to be the segmentation task (in image processing), it is supposed to be applied to a categorical dataset in which categories are the spatial patterns. The dataset consist of local climate zones over the entire region of Nagpur. There are 24 different local climate zone categories as per the supervised pixel based classification process (see Fig. 7a). Initially, the dataset is partitioned into grids and these grids are further segmented to form superpixels. The considered LCZ raster consist of 446,224 pixels (30 m resolution) which are transformed into 4489 grids (300 m resolution) and grids are further transformed into 7999 superpixels.

Figure 6 illustrates the process of super pixel formation implemented in DEALB. The basic unit considered for superpixel formation is a tile or a grid representing 300 m resolution. The grid verification result against the threshold parameter ‘T’ decides whether to further segment the grid or not. This threshold parameter checks the pattern variability in the grid and is set as 10% for the given dataset. Before the grid is segmented, the pixel contiguity in four sides of the grid (rightmost, leftmost, topmost and bottommost) is tested for the donation purpose. If the pixels are spatially connected and their pattern matches with the corresponding neighbor (Fig. 6c, they are donated and the remaining grid pixels were considered for segmentation purpose. For the donation purpose, 4 neighbors (left, right, top and bottom) are verified for the focusing grid. The grid with pattern variability less than ‘T’ (i.e. the grid contains the pattern variability but not spatially contiguous), is considered as a superpixel as shown in Fig. 6a, whereas the grid with a threshold greater than ‘T’ is tessellated into superpixels as shown in Fig. 6b.

Figure 7a shows the delineation of local climate zones for the given study area using the proposed methodology. The delineated LCZs are represented in a map encapsulating different color codes. We used the shapefile format to represent the LCZ map. There are twenty four different colors for twenty four LCZs. We emphasize that we use color only for visualization purpose and that the methodology is not based on color. The LCZ region boundaries, indicated by black color, enclose homogeneous spatial characteristics. Figure 7b maps the superpixels to color codes according to their homogeneity value (Shannon’s entropy (Eq. 1)). There are 7999 superpixels created. The range of homogeneity value for the superpixels is between 0 and 1.2. A smaller value (dark violet color) indicates greater homogeneous regions.

The LCZ boundaries (shown by black color in Fig. 7a) are quantitatively analyzed using two measures in the absence of any ground truth image: boundary pixels based purity index (BPPI Eq. (4)) and region based purity index (BRPI, Eq. (5)). Quality of the boundary is measured by similarity within the region and dissimilarity among the neighborhood regions of the considered region. BPPI measures the spatial pattern contrast along the boundaries of the region within the specified neighborhood. For the given example, the neighborhood of size 3 is selected while analyzing the boundary based spatial contrast. However, BRPI considers the spatial contrast of the considered region and its adjacent regions. A different spatial feature of focusing region and its adjacent regions validates the purity of the boundary. The metric BPPI and BRPI are calculated for each region for spatial dependability. Average values of these metrics over all regions are used to compare them effectively in locating boundaries. Table 3 summarizes the value statistics of both metrics for the given dataset and the Fig. 8 shows the BPPI and BRPI values for the given dataset. The quality of the regions formed is better represented by their homogeneity values (Fig. 7b) and boundary purity indices (Fig. 8a, b). In the literature (Jasiewicz et al., 2018), isolation is considered as the metric for quality in identifying similar and cohesive landscape patterns. Isolation tells that how each pattern is different from their surrounding pattern. Isolation is generally analyzed by Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) and a high value is preferred. In this paper, a pair-wise calculation of JSD (focusing region, adjacent region) is achieved for every region as shown in Fig. 8c. From the Fig. 8a–c, it is observed that the proposed boundary purity indices are working well in coordination with the traditional metric i.e. isolation.

We explain the rationality of both purity indexes by using the LCZ regions shown in Fig. 9. In Fig. 9, there are 4 different LCZ regions LCZ 1 (magenta), 2 (yellow), 3 (light pink) and 4 (blue). For the center region, LCZ 1, the boundary is drawn with red color and all the boundary pixels are represented by labels with the suffix B. In the boundary pixel based purity index (BPPI), the inside and outside spatial contrast for each boundary pixel within mentioned spatial neighborhood size is calculated. For example, the inside and outside spatial contrast for boundary pixel ‘P’ is given by pixels {1, 1, 1} and {4, 4, 4}. The Shannon’s entropy value is used to represent inside and outside spatial contrast. The inside spatial contrast for all boundary pixels of LCZ 1 is a vector [0, 0, 0, 0.918, 0.918, 0, 0, 0, 0.918, 0.918] of size 10. Similarly, the vector [0, 0, 0, 0.918, 0.918, 0, 0.918, 0, 0, 0.918] gives the outside spatial contrast of all boundary pixels. The next step is to average the difference in inside and outside spatial contrast and it is 0 for the boundary specified by LCZ 1. A zero value indicates the boundary is pure and it correctly separates the regions. In region based purity index (BRPI), the dissimilarity between center regions and its neighbouring members is used. In Fig. 9, the center region is represented by LCZ 1 (RC) and its neighbouring regions are LCZ 2 (R1), LCZ 3(R2), and LCZ 4 (R3). A pair-wise ((RC, R1), (RC, R2), (RC, R3)) dissimilarity values are calculated and for the example given in Fig. 9 is a vector [0.05, 0.04, 0.03]. When they are averaged with a number of neighbouring regions, the BRPI became 0.04 which is acceptable but a little bit more than BPPI.

Comparison With Other Methodologies

To the best of our knowledge, the problem of localizing LCZ boundaries is unnoticed by researchers. Hence, due to the lack of identifying regions and their boundaries with respect to the LCZ concept, we compared the proposed methodology with two possible techniques focusing on spatial grouping/database. One is regionalization based complex object based image analysis (COBIA) (Stepinski et al., 2015). This method is selected for comparison as it utilizes similar kind of input (i.e. pixel classified raster image) used in our proposed algorithm. The other method selected based on clustering mechanism is the traditional density based spatial clustering of applications with noise. This method is selected because it considers the spatial cohesiveness constraint on which the proposed algorithm is focused.

COBIA is notably suited to delineate landscape types present in large geospatial image. In COBIA, the classified image is reduced to regular sized grids. The grid is nothing but the block of pattern-bearing pixels. A histogram of pattern features is used to represent the grid. To achieve homogeneous regions, the grids are further segmented using a non-hierarchical greedy region growing segmentation algorithm by using distance space constraint. Jenson-Shannon-divergence (JSD) is used to measure the distance between adjacent grids. As the segmentation algorithm region grows locally, hence it is possible that two or more disjoint segments contain identical patterns. Hence, hierarchical clustering with Ward’s linkage is utilized to cluster segments into final landscape units of unique patterns.

We also applied the conventional DBSCAN to the LCZ classified raster. DBSCAN assumes that the cluster in data space is a continuous region of high point density and is separated by a contiguous region of lower density. It identifies the clusters in the large geospatial database by looking at the density of the data points and is robust to outliers. Unlike the other clustering algorithms like K-means, it does not necessitate the number of clusters beforehand. The necessary parameters for DBSCAN are epsilon and minPoints. Epsilon represents the radius of the circle to be created around each data point for the density check and minPoints is the minimum number of points required inside that circle to decide whether the point is a core point or a border point. For the present paper, the value of epsilon is set to 1 and the value utilized for minimum points is 4 representing 120 m neighborhood. After applying the DBSCAN algorithm spatially dispersed clusters were formed and to delineate these clusters, the proposed boundary tracing algorithm was applied.

Figure 10 shows the result of the proposed DELAB and comparable methodologies (COBIA and DBSCAN) for the LCZ classified raster. The COBIA was implemented with an initial grid size of 10X10 corresponding to 300 m, and the conventional DBSCAN was considered with parameters eps = 1 and minPoints = 4 (equivalent 120 m distance). Though DBSCAN creates the clusters of similar but spatially connected pixels, but it doesn’t provides the mechanism of delineation. Hence, to the output of DBSCAN, the proposed DEALB algorithm was applied. Figure 10 shows the output maps generated from three methodologies. Shannon’s entropy is used to compare the homogeneity of the regions and is calculated for every region in the image. The statistics about a number of regions created and their average homogeneity values for each method is presented in Table 4. We can see that the proposed algorithm DEALB produces less number of regions (1178) for the same dataset than COBIA (3058) and DBSCAN (7218). Also the average homogeneity has increased (average entropy of 0.1866) in the results given by our proposed algorithm. If we look at the map in Fig. 10b, which shows the regionalization result for COBIA, we get a number of small regions. Such geographical small regions need dissolution in adjacent regions as shown in Fig. 10a. The existing methods create the homogeneous regions but a post-regionalization step to identify the landscape name for the region is required. Hence, we applied a post-regionalization step to identify landscape labels corresponding to each region formed by COBIA and DBSCAN. In our proposed algorithm, a post-regionalization step is not required and the landscape names are attached to regions parallel as shown in Fig. 7a. To increase the interpretability of the maps generated by all the three methods, we have used the same color code representation to represent the LCZs in the map. From Fig. 10a, c, it is observed that the structure of maps generated from DEALB and DBSCAN + DEALB resembles identity. The map generated by using COBIA (Fig. 10b) shows much number of small regions.

Discussion

Mathematically, regionalization is an optimization problem to maximize within-region similarity and inter-region dissimilarity. All the state-of-the art methodologies (Aydin et al., 2021; Carvalho et al., 2009; Duque et al., 2007; Felzenszwalb, 2004; Jasiewicz et al., 2018; Openshaw, 1973; Sharma et al., 2008; Shih & Cheng, 2005; Yuan et al., 2015) give emphasis on generating homogeneous regions with spatial continuity constraint whereas the aim of the present paper is to not only identify homogeneous and spatially continuous regions, but to locate their boundaries. In landscape localization, identifying boundary using regionalization is beneficial over convex boundary tracing because spatial distribution of the same landscape pattern exists at different locations in the area considered. For example, spatial distribution of landscape patterns ‘3’ and ‘6’ are as shown in Fig. 11a. If the convex boundary (red color in Fig. 11b) is drawn covering all spatial points, the outliers are also considered while tracing the region boundary as shown in Fig. 11b. Also, the drawn boundary will embed the portion of different unnecessary landscapes. Hence, to overcome this issue, the proposed solution segregates the spatially dispersed regions of the same landscape pattern and then localizes their boundaries. Boundaries (shown with black colour in Fig. 11c) of the same landscape regions after the proposed regionalization process are as shown in Fig. 11c.

The proposed boundary locating algorithm for the categorical raster dataset specifying local climate zone categories for the Nagpur region results in 1178 homogeneous regions from 446,224 raster cells which is less than the regions formed by traditional methods COBIA (3058) and DBSCAN (7218). The proposed algorithm is beneficial for categorical raster over COBIA and DBSCAN with respect to the following features—(1) most traditional regionalization algorithms necessitate some parameters to be identified prior, such as the number of regions. This requires expert knowledge about the structure of the region of interest. Instead, the proposed algorithm automatically identifies homogeneous regions without explicitly specifying the value for the number of region parameter. (2) A landscape map with regions mapped with their landscape categories is beneficial for a domain expert to analyse the results and validate the correctness. With COBIA and DBSCAN, a post-regionalization step is necessary to map the landscape category to the region based on the contribution of pixels inside the region whereas the proposed DEALB algorithm’s outcome assigns the landscape category to the region and is represented as the landscape map (see Fig. 7a). This map represents the regions with specific color code for each landscape category. Such representation is helpful for an urban expert to analyse the results, to validate the correctness, and take various urban planning decisions.

Further, to validate the correctness of LCZ regions in the generated map by using our proposed algorithm, we overlaid the LCZ map on the Google Earth Explorer platform. After performing this step, the experts are able to verify the landscape characteristics within each region as well as the correctness of the LCZ map generated. For some randomly selected regions/superpixels the snapshot view of their ground-truth landscapes are shown in Fig. 12. Figure 12a represents the 5 randomly selected regions out of which 4 (Fig. 12c–f) are individual regions and 1 (Fig. 12b) shows the embedded (LCZ G is inside LCZ C) regions. These regions correspond to LCZ C, G, 3 and 23. The expanded ground-truth images of these regions are depicted in Fig. 12b–f. From the Fig. 12b, it can be observed that, our proposed algorithm’s delineated map is able to visualize the embedded LCZs efficiently and effectively.

As mentioned in the introduction section of this paper, there exist only a few post-classification techniques in the context of LCZ (Demuzere et al., 2020, 2021). The primary focus of these techniques is the smoothness of pixel based LCZ classification result rather than identifying LCZ borders. One of the post-classification methods mentioned in the LCZ literature is majority filter proposed by the standard WUDAPT (Demuzere et al., 2021). We compared the result of our proposed algorithm with the traditional WUDAPT majority filter outcome on the LCZ categorical raster for the Nagpur region. The comparative results are shown in Fig. 13. The output represented by majority filter remains the raster but smoothened at some level whereas the output of the proposed DEALB algorithm is the next level representation in terms of polygons (shapefile). Such a representation is helpful in geographical scientific queries.

Novelty

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study of identifying homogeneous LCZ regions and delineating their boundaries. It is a post-classification methodology for generating high level representation of LCZs in the region of interest. Studies using LCZ concept include mapping LCZ using satellite input data (Bechtel et al., 2016; Demuzere et al., 2019, 2021), land surface temperature (LST) correlation (Cai et al., 2018; Geletič et al., 2016), outdoor thermal comfort analysis using LCZ concept (Lau et al., 2019), intra-inter air temperature variability within LCZ (Daniel et al., 2017; Justus et al., 2018) etc. There is a scarcity of research in the post-classification methodology for better visualizing the LCZ map. Also, the proposed method reduces the LCZ classified result to a coarser level which is required by urban experts for various urban planning decisions. Though the proposed method is tested for the LCZ categorical dataset but it can be easily utilised for other categorical rasters as well.

Conclusion

LCZ map is a proven tool for planning better climate resilient cities. Multiple studies have used the LCZ map as a tool to investigate into various aspects of urban climate and subsequently have used it for planning, mitigation and adaptation strategies. There exist multiple studies on LCZ mapping at different resolutions using a variety of satellite datasets but no attention has been paid towards better representation of the LCZ classified outcome. For a required resolution, identifying LCZs, clustering adjacent similar LCZs enclosed with boundary line are the basic requirements for generating a high level representation of the LCZ map. Hence, our purpose is to propose an algorithm for locating boundaries of landscapes represented as local climate zones and show its effectiveness. The basic unit of computation is defined as a superpixel which is formed by aggregating pixels in the local neighborhood by using the directional edge concept. The local climate zones or regions are interpreted by clustering superpixels. We also perform a quantitative assessment of boundaries using two proposed metrics, boundary pixel based purity index (BPPI) and region based purity index (BRPI). These values are calculated for each region formed in the process. As our proposed methodology DEALB deals with forming homogeneous regions, we compared it with traditional regionalization techniques for spatial datasets such as COBIA and DBSCAN. In general, the proposed DEALB produces less number of regions (1178) for the study area as compared to COBIA (3058) and DBSCAN (7218). As DBSCAN deals with only clustering, we further applied DEALB to DBSCAN output. Using this, we recorded the highest homogeneity (lowest average entropy value 0.1638) for the study area over the DEALB (average entropy value 0.1866) and COBIA (least homogeneity with average entropy value 0.7864). Presently the methodology is tested for example dataset i.e. LCZ categorical raster dataset for Nagpur, Maharashtra. The future work will extend the methodology for large geospatial raster including implementation of heterogeneity analysis.
