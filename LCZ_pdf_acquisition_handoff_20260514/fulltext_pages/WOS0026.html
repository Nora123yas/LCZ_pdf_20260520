Abstract

Surface Urban Heat Islands significantly deteriorate urban sustainability and livability. Effective ways to monitor and mitigate this phenomenon are gaining importance among environment researchers. In order to lessen the extreme heat waves of tropical cities, an understanding of the spatial features affecting urban micro-climate is needed. Local climate zones can provide the much needed framework for city level climate studies. This paper presented satellite imagery-based workflow to identify critical zones within a city, using the spatial thermal characteristics. The results brought out the critical thermal scenario in summer, for two mega cities of India. Prolonged heat trapping resulted in 13.99% nocturnal heat island cover in the New Delhi region and 22.26% in the Kolkata City region. Moderate to strong heat stress was observed for central city areas, even at night time. Surface temperature variation within local climate zones of a city reflected significant rise in temperature over certain urban pockets due to building morphology. Over a distance of 12 km from vegetated land to low-rise dense built-up, a drastic 8.76 °C temperature rise was noted on a summer night. The temperature patterns were further verified with seasonal surface albedo mapping. Based on areal distribution, location, thermal range and contribution in heat island formation, sustainable planning strategies for each local climate zone were laid out. The study aimed to achieve an optimum cooling level for given urban structures and also highlighted the potential thermal environment changes faced by suburban regions.

Introduction

Critical degradation in the urban environment has been observed in most of the major cities worldwide, especially over the past few decades. Abrupt changes in urban climate reflected through the anomalous temperature trends have become an important concern. Various researchers have reported the unrestrained conversion of the natural land cover into built-up (Ren 2015; Dutta et al. 2018). Several influencing factors like presence of excessive concrete surfaces, lack of evapotranspiration, increased anthropogenic activities, disturbed wind flow pattern due to irregular urban geometry, etc., have led to increased air temperature in city regions and intensification of Urban Heat Islands (UHI) (Pandey et al. 2012; Yu et al. 2019). Oke (1973) defined the phenomenon of UHI as the occurrence of relatively higher temperature over cities, in comparison with their rural counterparts. The occurrence of UHI can be more pronounced long after sunset due to prolonged heat trapping in congested urban sectors associated with concentrated anthropogenic activities (Dutta et al. 2019). The advent of satellite imageries aided spatio-temporal processing of the available diurnal and nocturnal thermal data. This enabled researchers to quantify Surface Urban Heat Island (SUHI) and include it as an essential segment in the studies related to urban climate change assessment. The presence of diurnal and nocturnal SUHIs has been verified globally by researchers using the spatial profile of Land Surface Temperature (LST) (Tiangco et al. 2008; Hua et al. 2012; Soltani and Sharifi 2017; Sultana and Satyanarayana 2020).

The extreme heat events in city regions are known to cause increased mortality and heat stress related illness (Son et al. 2017). The intense SUHI condition generates higher demand of cooling. This eventually leads to huge power consumption through air conditioning systems in the SUHI affected urban areas (Magli et al. 2015; Rossi et al. 2016). In general, SUHI is a threat to the sustainability of the city landscape, urban tourism and social welfare (Gautam et al. 2016). Hence, it seems mandatory to identify, monitor and characterize the SUHI to successfully alleviate or adapt to elevated urban LST. However, identification of SUHI will not be adequate for quantitative assessment of the heat stress imposed upon city dwellers. Thus, the globally acknowledged Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI) (Xu et al. 2019) is often adopted in addition to the SUHI analysis. The UTCI can directly reflect the urban health scenarios with respect to different LST classes (Błazejczyk et al. 2010; Ndetto and Matzarakis 2015). Studies considering the mutual interactions between SUHI and UTCI can provide perspectives into urban LST adjustments, thereby protecting the vulnerable urban population.

A thorough understanding of the urban spatial layout was needed to decrease the extent of SUHI cover, the intensity of SUHI and to raise outdoor thermal comfort. The Land Use Land Cover (LULC) pattern has been widely adopted to comprehend urban climate characteristics (Yang et al. 2019; Dutta et al. 2021). Most of the LULC schemes classify the entire urban built-up into two or three sub-classes and therefore can give the urban–rural LST differences only. Hence, LULC classes lowered the SUHI mitigation options at the urban planning level. There was a need to address the spatial attributes of LST within changing urban morphology. In order to overcome this limitation, Stewart and Oke (2012) provided the concept of Local Climate Zones (LCZs) for urban micro-climate studies. This LCZ classification scheme used numerous urban morphological properties like sky view factor, aspect ratio, building surface fraction, impervious surface fraction, pervious surface fraction, height of roughness elements and terrain roughness. Extending more than a few kilometers, each LCZ class represented landscape with homogeneous urban functions and forms. The urban built-up form was expected to significantly alter local micro-climate. Hence, LCZ classes should be used as major elements to evaluate SUHI. LCZ classification scheme also made possible the comparison between urban micro-climates. Another key parameter in controlling surface thermal characteristics is the albedo (Liang 2001). Albedo directly reflects the balance of radiation at the surface and affects the local climate (da Silva et al. 2016).

Various researchers have attempted to perceive urban climate using LCZs in recent times (Chen et al. 2020; Badaro-saliba et al. 2021; Cilek and Cilek 2021). Several of these research works have highlighted the problem of SUHI for a metropolis or megacity (Cardoso and Amorim 2018; Wang et al. 2018; Cai et al. 2019). However, such single city-based case studies can only provide intrinsic spatial patterns or discrepancies of SUHI intensity, owing to the unique urban characteristics of an individual city in terms of the size, building density and land-use classification. In this regard, it was imperative to analyze the urban micro-climate variation for two densely populated tropical mega cities, namely New Delhi and Kolkata, having different geographical settings. It was felt that the critical suburban areas were needed to be identified for these cities due to the ongoing rapid urban sprawl. Satellite town planning policies in such locations must incorporate local climate responsive urban designs. Hence, a regional scale of spatial extent was selected in the present study for the analysis of inter-urban and intra-urban LST profiles.

The comparison between summer and winter SUHI intensity and extent was used in this study to identify the critical season in SUHI formation. Satellite images of April and May 2019 were used of summer pattern, and images of December 2019 were utilized to obtain winter data. This study aimed to develop a thorough methodology for linking SUHI (diurnal and nocturnal) patterns with outdoor thermal comfort zones (diurnal and nocturnal) using a local LCZ classification scheme. The objective of this study was to understand the differences in the thermal behavior of inter-urban LCZ sites for two different cities with distinct urban characteristics. Potential threat areas to sustainability and urban health were identified for each selected city. The detailed approach for LCZ classification outlined in this study will help urban planners to improve key physical characteristics contributing to the livability of these cities, such as land use distribution, urban built form patterns, quality of public spaces and conservation of natural environments. The effect of particular building morphology on urban climate was further validated with spatial mapping of albedo. This study developed the urban cooling strategies specific to each city. This will contribute to the handling of SUHI, as per the local conditions, using the built environment in various locations.

Study area

New Delhi

The capital of India, New Delhi, was taken as the first study region. The city is situated on the banks of the river Yamuna. It is the largest commercial hub of northern India with a population density of 11,320 people per km2 (Directorate of Census Operations 2011). The study area taken for analyzing the spatial contrast in LST and LCZ pattern for the city and its surroundings is shown in Fig. 1a. The New Delhi city region stretched from 28° 25′ N to 28° 58′ N and from 76° 48′ E to 77° 35′ E. The total area extended over a vast 6067.01 km2. The hot, semiarid climate and continental location of New Delhi have resulted in intense heat waves, presence of thermal stress and peak power usage during recent summer seasons.

The urban setting of New Delhi contains a mix of old and new built-up regions. The commercial and business center or the Central Business District (CBD) of this city is present in and around Connaught Place. New commercial and residential blocks have grown and expanded from this CBD region. Several historical and tourist places are located in different parts of the city, including a few world heritage sites. The prime urban centers are marked in Fig. 1a.

Kolkata

Kolkata city was taken as the second study region. Located at the banks of river Ganga, Kolkata was the former capital of India. It is currently one of the major port and commercial center of east India and hence densely populated (24,306 people per km2) (Directorate of Census Operations West Bengal 2011). Similar to New Delhi, a bounding box containing Kolkata and its immediate neighborhood was taken. The spatial extent of the study area was from 22° 27′ N to 22° 40′ N and 88° 14′ E to 88° 34′ E, as shown in Fig. 1b. The total area covered was 848.97 km2. Geographically Kolkata falls under tropical wet and dry climate.

Kolkata is a typical mono centric city with the CBD present over the Dharmatala area. Unlike Delhi, the commercial core of Kolkata is old and unplanned. In contrast to that, the outskirts are currently being used to develop new residential plots. Severe conversion of wetlands and vegetation into built-up has taken place, especially in the eastern direction. Major satellite towns like Salt Lake City, New Town and Rajarhat have grown in this region. Hence, the study area included a vast extent in the eastern side of Kolkata city. The major urban centers of the Kolkata city region are marked in Fig. 1b.

Materials

The LCZ classes were obtained by processing Landsat 8 satellite imageries. Recent LCZ and urban micro-climate patterns were only substantial for current city planning. Hence, data of the year 2019 were used in this study. As per Indian Meteorological Department, the months of March, April and May are identified as pre-monsoon or summer months for India, while the winter season consists of December, January and February months. Cloud-free data of both summer and winter months were taken for SUHI analysis. Day time LST pattern was extracted from thermal bands of Landsat 8. For night time thermal images MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer) data were used. The spatial resolution of day and night time images varied. There were very limited sources of readily available night time images. Among them, MODIS images were the most preferred ones. In this work, the goal was to map the critical city areas and extract location based data. No pixel-based comparison was made. Therefore, the difference in spatial resolution did not make any impact on the results. The detail of these satellite images is listed in Table 1.

Materials and methods

The methodology proposed in this study is presented through a flowchart in Fig. 2.

Urban LST and SUHI mapping

Day time LST (LSTD)

The spatial pattern of day time LST was generated from the thermal band of Landsat 8. The conversion of pixel Digital Numbers (DN) into blackbody temperature (Kelvin) was carried out by following the procedure mentioned in the research article by U.S. Geological Survey (2016). This blackbody temperature in Kelvin lacks spectral emissivity corrections. A correction, suggested by Artis and Carnahan (1982), was applied to compute emissivity adjusted surface temperature in Kelvin as,

$$ {\mathbf{T}}_{{\mathbf{S}}} = \frac{{{\mathbf{T}}_{{\mathbf{B}}} }}{{1 + \left( {{{\varvec{\uplambda}}} \times \frac{{{\mathbf{T}}_{{\mathbf{B}}} }}{{{\varvec{\upalpha}}}}} \right){\mathbf{ln}} {{\varvec{\upvarepsilon}}}}} $$
(1)

where TS = surface radiant temperature in Kelvin; TB = blackbody temperature in Kelvin; λ = wavelength of emitted radiance which in this case was 11.5 µm; α = hc/K; h = Planck’s constant; c = velocity of light; K = Boltzmann constant; ε = surface emissivity.

The land surface emissivity was estimated through Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) using the following equation,

$$ {{\varvec{\upvarepsilon}}} = 1.0094 + 0.0047{\mathbf{ln}} \times {\mathbf{NDVI}} $$
(2)

The Kelvin temperature is finally converted to day time surface LST in degree Celsius as,

$$ {\mathbf{LST}}_{{\mathbf{D}}} = {\mathbf{T}}_{{\mathbf{S}}} - 273.15 $$
(3)

Night time LST (LSTN)

MODIS night time thermal images were adjusted with the scaling factor to obtain nocturnal LST pattern in Kelvin as per the following formula (Wan 2013),

$$ {\mathbf{LST}}_{{\mathbf{N}}} \left( {\mathbf{K}} \right) = {\mathbf{DN}} \times 0.02 $$
(4)

Similar to day time LST profile, the Kelvin LST values of the night time maps were also converted into degree Celsius.

SUHI zoning

SUHI represents extremely high LST zones within cities. To identify these zones, both day time and night time LST maps were categorized into five classes. The categorization of LST maps was based on the Jenks Natural Breaks algorithm (North 2009). As per this algorithm, classes were created based on natural grouping inherent within the data. The natural breaks were data specific classification, and the grouping was not affected by season, time or place of data source. Individual values in each class were systematically assigned to adjacent classes by adjusting the class boundaries. This iterative process ended with minimizing the inter-class variance and maximizing the intra-class variance. Hence, it was considered to be suitable for LST classification and SUHI identification. In this study, the five LST classes adopted are given below:

  • Very low temperature

  • Low temperature

  • Moderate temperature

  • High temperature

  • Very high temperature

The very high temperature zones were identified as diurnal or nocturnal SUHIs for each study region. The SUHI zones were mapped for both summer and winter seasons. This helped to assert whether the SUHI problem is present for all types of seasons or not. The extent and intensity of seasonal SUHI was noted. Based on the results, the critical season for SUHI generation in this tropical scenario was determined. All other application based on the urban thermal environment was further carried out using the LST data of the critical season.

Mapping of urban thermal comfort zones

The LST maps (both day and night) were classified in different outdoor heat stress zones. The global standards given by Xu et al. (2019) were used for defining the LST ranges for the UTCI classification, as shown in Table 2.

LCZ classification

The mapping of LCZ classes was carried out as per the guidelines provided by WUDAPT (World Urban Database and Access Portal Tools). The classification is a semi-automatic procedure. The training areas for each LCZ class were user defined and digitized in the Google Earth software. There are 17 types of LCZ classes. Out of these, the first 10 (LCZ1 to LCZ10) are the built-up classes and the next 7 categories (LCZA to LCZG) represent natural land cover (Stewart and Oke 2012). Around 15 training samples (polygons) were created for each LCZ class. The Landsat image bands were stacked to produce a composite image. The panchromatic band was used for pan sharpening of the composite image (Liu et al. 2017). Thus the resolutions of LCZ maps were improved to 15 m. This stacked image and the training areas were utilized for obtaining LCZ classes (Wang et al. 2018). Supervised classification with the Maximum Likelihood algorithm was applied in this study.

The LCZ scheme of classification was slightly modified for Kolkata City. An additional class of wetland (LCZH) was included in the case of the Kolkata region. Wetlands have a significant effect on the urban thermal environment. This class was created because wetlands were covering a considerable area in eastern Kolkata.

After the LCZ classifications, a total of 500 random points were generated for each study area. The accuracy of LCZ classification was assessed using these 500 points. Land cover characteristics observed through Google Earth were considered as the reference of validation. The correspondence between classified map and reference image was checked through confusion matrices (Dewan and Yamaguchi 2009). The overall accuracy, producer’s accuracy, user’s accuracy and Kappa statistic were computed to ensure the LCZ maps were reliable for further applications in the study (Shao and Wu 2008).

Spatial profiling of urban/non-urban ratio

The spatial pattern of urban built-up changes remarkably from CBD region toward city fringes. This spatial pattern is expected to affect urban LST characteristics, as dense built-up will enhance the heat trapping. The gradual transition in land cover categories was checked by creating buffer rings. Circular buffer zones were prepared by starting from the city center. The buffer distance was consecutively increased by 5 km for the New Delhi region. In total, 8 circular rings were generated to cover the 1st study area. A different buffer distance is taken for the Kolkata study area due to the smaller geographical extent. Here, the buffer distance was consecutively increased by 2 km, which resulted in 12 buffer rings.

The LCZ maps were clipped for all the buffer zones separately. Two parameters of urbanization were extracted from the LCZ class information for each successive ring. The first parameter was the dense urban to non-urban ratio (DU/NU). It was simply computed as a ratio between the area covered by LCZ urban classes with a dense mix of buildings (LCZ1 + LCZ2 + LCZ3 + LCZ7 + LCZ10) and LCZ classes of the natural landscape (LCZA + LCZB + LCZC + LCZD + LCZE + LCZF + LCZG). The second parameter was the open urban to non-urban ratio (OU/NU). It was a ratio between area covered by LCZ urban classes with openly arranged buildings (LCZ4 + LCZ5 + LCZ6 + LCZ8 + LCZ9) and area covered by LCZ classes of the natural landscape. The value of urban to non-urban ratio above 1 signified greater urbanization at the expense of a natural land cover. The ratio value below 1 implied that the natural landscape has a greater influence on the local environment than the urban areas. The transition in these ratios from the city center to the outer boundary was utilized to understand the SUHI pattern.

Association between LCZ classes and LST variation

Analysis of Inter-LCZ LST

The rise and fall in LST were linked with particular land cover classes by extracting LCZ wise LST data. The day time LST pattern has better resolution (30 m) and is expected to show greater spatial variation than the nocturnal data (1 km). These diurnal LST maps were clipped separately for each LCZ class. The mean, maximum and minimum LST of all LCZ classes were plotted.

Contribution of LCZs in SUHI zones

The inter-LCZ temperature analysis can explore land cover class wise temperature distribution while considering the entire range (from minimum to maximum) of LST. A single outlier pixel from the LST maps can affect the results. It does not directly quantify each LCZ’s contribution in SUHI creation. This was specifically looked into using Distribution Index (DI) method. DI is a ratio which is computed as (Zhao 2018),

$$ {\mathbf{DI}} = \frac{{{\mathbf{SUHI}}_{{{\mathbf{LCZi}}}} /{\mathbf{A}}_{{{\mathbf{LCZi}}}} }}{{{\mathbf{SUHI}}/{\mathbf{A}}}} $$
(5)

where SUHILCZi = SUHI area cover in a particular LCZ class; ALCZi = total area cover of the same class; SUHI = SUHI area for entire study region; A = area of entire study region.

Based on the DI, the percent contribution of each LCZ class in SUHI formation was computed.

Spatial mapping of surface albedo

The surface albedo value was determined using Landsat 8 bands as per the procedure laid out by da Silva et al. (2016). The technique applies the monochromatic reflectance of the Landsat 8 reflective bands (2 to 7) in a linear combination, as

$$ {{\varvec{\upalpha}}}_{{{\mathbf{toa}}}} = {\mathbf{p}}_{2} \times {\mathbf{r}}_{2} + {\mathbf{p}}_{3} \times {\mathbf{r}}_{3} + {\mathbf{p}}_{4} \times {\mathbf{r}}_{4} + {\mathbf{p}}_{5} \times {\mathbf{r}}_{5} + {\mathbf{p}}_{6} \times {\mathbf{r}}_{6} + {\mathbf{p}}_{7} \times {\mathbf{r}}_{7} $$
(6)

where rb is the reflectance of each of the bands and pb is the weight assigned to each band. To determine pb, a solar constant was computed using both reflectance and radiance of each band (da Silva et al. 2016). The value of each pb was obtained by the ratio between the solar constant of that band and the sum of all the values of solar constants used in the calculation of the albedo. The surface albedo map was prepared for both summer and winter seasons. The major critical SUHI areas, seasonal change areas in terms of SUHI and low thermal areas were all validated by comparing the albedo values.

LCZ class wise SUHI mitigation strategies

LCZ classes provide 10 categories within urban land cover itself. All these categories have different levels of contribution in raising or decreasing urban LST. Also, due to their distinct spatial structures, similar SUHI mitigation strategies cannot be applied to them. Hence, the final proposals of sustainable city planning incorporated results from all the applied methodologies. The suggested cooling strategies were based on the extent and location of any LCZ class, built-up characteristics (especially building heights) of a class, LST range and contribution to SUHI area cover.

Results and discussion

Identification of SUHI areas from LST maps

New Delhi city region

The spatial pattern of summer day time LST and the identified zones of SUHI for the New Delhi city region are shown in Fig. 3a. Simultaneously, the summer night time LST profile and the SUHIs are represented in Fig. 3b.

The LST profile of winter month for the year 2019 is shown in Fig. 3c, d for diurnal and nocturnal conditions, respectively. It was notable that for New Delhi mapping of diurnal SUHI was not suitable. The presence of bare soil and rocks outside the city boundary has resulted in higher LST in these regions than in the city (validated through LCZs in Sect. 5.3.1). The LST pattern for both seasons depicted the absence of diurnal SUHI over the central city. Similar presence of negative SUHI effect in few Indian cities has been detected by Shastri et al. (2017). In contrast to this, the identification of SUHI was possible from nocturnal LST map for both summer and winter. From Fig. 3b, d, the presence of SUHI can be clearly observed over central New Delhi, on both sides of river Yamuna (A and D). SUHI were also prominent in western (B) and southern (C) city areas. Corresponding to Fig. 1a, it can be noted that all major residential and commercial sections of New Delhi city are lying over the central SUHI zones (A and D region). Even the world heritage sites are vulnerable to heat stress as they are present within the extension of SUHIs. In night time for both seasons, the stretch of river Yamuna showed moderate to high LST. This was due to the coarser spatial resolution of nocturnal data (1 km) and the domination of dense built-up on both banks of river Yamuna. The high LST of these city centers resulted in raising the temperature for these particular pixels located over river area. In a continental location like New Delhi city region, the day time LST was high for sandy, rocky areas and also impervious urban surfaces; but after sunset the LST of the natural or rural landscape reduced prominently. While the accumulated heat over urban pockets remained much higher than the surroundings. The relative LST of the urban area hence was markedly greater than the immediate neighborhood at night. This led to the severe SUHI scenario at night time in terms of both areal extent and intensity. Comparing the seasonal LST pattern, the critical thermal season for the study was identified. The total area covered by SUHI on summer night was 849 km2, but in winter night the area covered by SUHI shrank to 689 km2. The SUHI intensity was checked between the densely built Shahdara area (E) and the Hindon Airport region (F) of sparse built-up. Within this distance of only 12 km, the summer nocturnal SUHI intensity was 8.76 °C. From Fig. 3d the decrease in the higher LST region in the winter month was evident in the southern fringes of New Delhi’s boundary (G). In winter, the SUHI was more concentrated only over New Delhi city. The nocturnal winter SUHI intensity between the same two locations of Shahdara and Hindon Airport region was 5.87 °C, nearly 3 °C less than that of summer night. This outlined how critical the problem of prolonged heat trapping was in summer for this mega city. The results established that there was a strong presence of SUHI even in winter nights, but the thermal environment of the New Delhi region was more hazardous in summer.

Kolkata city region

The diurnal and nocturnal LST pattern of summer 2019, along with the SUHI zones for the Kolkata city region, are shown in Fig. 4a, b, respectively. The winter 2019 LST profiles for day and night are represented through Fig. 4c, d, respectively.

Unlike Delhi, both the diurnal and nocturnal LST pattern for Kolkata depicted extensive thermal hotspots over the central city region (A). These SUHIs were bordered by the following high LST class. While the areas near the study region boundary were marked with the very low temperature class. In summer (2019) day time, SUHI covered 79.13 km2 of the Kolkata city region. Other than the CBD region, SUHI also has developed on the opposite bank of river Ganga (B). A similar LST pattern on both sides of the river Ganga was also mapped in the works of Gazi and Mondal (2018), but the urban sprawl toward the east of Kolkata was not highlighted. It can be observed from Fig. 4a that zones of high LST were spreading in linear form in the east of Kolkata city (C and D). This indicated the intensification of SUHI in satellite towns within the near future. From Fig. 4b, nocturnal summer SUHIs were found to be more magnified than day time. The extension of this very high LST class was about 189 km2 at night. A larger difference of LST can be observed between the urban and non-urban region during the night time than the day. While day time LST pattern has mixed effect of radiation from sun and urban surface characteristics; nocturnal pattern particularly represented the excessive heat trapping in dense urban sectors. The extremity of anthropogenic effects was hence reflected by night time LST and SUHI pattern. The effect of urban sectors in retaining heat was further validated with surface albedo characteristics (Sect. 5.7.2). Similar to New Delhi, for nocturnal LST pattern it was noted that the pixels over river Ganga were influenced by the high-LST dense built-up located at the river banks. In the case of the winter season, the SUHI extension reduced significantly also for the Kolkata region. The day time SUHI area cover shrunk to 38.26 km2 and the night time SUHI covered 134 km2. The SUHI intensity for the Kolkata city region was checked by comparing the LST of the CBD area (A) with the green recreational area of Victoria Memorial (E). The day time SUHI intensity fell to 4.06 °C in winter, from 5.41 °C in summer. In the case of the nocturnal scenario, SUHI intensity in winter was 1.17 °C, while in summer it was 2.14 °C.

Comparing the results of two mega cities, it could be concluded that night time SUHI was more distinctly present over a larger city area. As both cities belonged to tropical regions, the SUHI extension and intensity were clearly more critical in summer season, making urgent monitoring of summer SUHI and formulation of mitigation strategies. Hence, the further results were deduced based on the summer LST pattern of both cities.

Spatial profile of outdoor Urban Thermal Comfort Index (UTCI)

New Delhi city region

The day time outdoor thermal comfort zones for New Delhi are shown in Fig. 5a. While Fig. 3a did not identify the SUHI locations in the day time, the UTCI mapping reflected extreme heat waves over New Delhi in summer. The entire central city area witnessed strong heat stress. Only within the boundary of New Delhi city, 630.44 km2 area was covered by the zone of strong heat stress. The surrounding region of higher LST was a part of a very strong heat stress zone. The night time UTCI map is shown in Fig. 5b. As the LST drops after sunset, the area in the proximity of the city boundary reflected no heat stress at all. Still, New Delhi showed moderate heat stress even at night time. It can be deduced that New Delhi city is experiencing a hazardous urban micro-climate at both day and night. Lack of thermal comfort in certain points of New Delhi city was also verified through Physiological Effective Temperature (PET) index, from the experiments carried out by Manavvi and Rajasekar (2019) in the summer days of 2017. The major urban locations from Fig. 1a were overlaid on the nocturnal outdoor thermal comfort map. The severity of prolonged heat stress on a summer night was expressed as all major urban centers belonging to New Delhi city and Noida region were part of moderate heat stress zone.

Kolkata city region

The day time and night time LST maps categorized as per UTCI zones for Kolkata are shown in Fig. 6a, b, respectively. For the day time map, an extensive urban area of 210.48 km2 belonged to the zone of strong heat stress, while almost the entire remaining study area experienced moderate heat stress. Only a few pixels from the dense urban built-up were marked with very strong heat stress. The spatial cover of this category was insignificant (1.01 km2). Around wetlands of eastern region and along the river Ganga no day time thermal stress can be seen, but the ‘no thermal stress’ zone was not continuous along the river channel. This pattern was observed precisely because of high building density at the central city region on both sides of river Ganga. The proximity to dense urban sectors from the river channel affected the thermal characteristics extracted from satellite images. Since, the images have defined spatial resolution, the LST of every pixel was affected by dominant land cover feature. Hence, certain patches along the channel depicted the presence of moderate heat stress at day time. The urban thermal environment was expected to depict much less intensity at night time. Still, moderate heat stress zones were present all over the city area, while the outskirts reflected no heat stress at all. This simply reflected the effect of anthropogenic activities and severity of heat trapping at city centers. Locations of major urban places of Kolkata from Fig. 1b were checked with respect to the nocturnal UTCI map. Except for Eco Park, all points witnessed moderate stress in summer nights. Comparing to New Delhi, it can be said that the moderate heat stress zones covered greater portions of land in Kolkata. The reason was likely to be the poorly planned sectors of the city and coastal location of Kolkata. This geographical setting prevented the significant fall in LST from day to night, unlike New Delhi. Hence, from the results, it can be assessed that night time heat stress was a more severe problem for tropical hot and humid cities.

Mapping of local climatic zones (LCZ)

New Delhi city region

The map showing 17 LCZ classes for the New Delhi region is shown in Fig. 7. Before further application of the LCZ classes in SUHI understanding, the accuracy of classification was assessed. The confusion matrix for New Delhi city region based on 500 random points depicted overall accuracy of 86.4%. The computed value of the Kappa statistic was 0.857. Both the values indicated that the classification of LCZs was reliable. The distribution of LCZ classes displayed that the central city region was mostly covered by compact mid-rise and open low-rise buildings. Open low-rise buildings covered a vast 849.38 km2 area. Among the other classes, large low-rise (940.99 km2) and sparsely built urban (641.70 km2) had high area cover. Overall, in New Delhi city region, the open arrangement of buildings dominated the dense mix of built-up. The distinct similarity in spatial pattern of compact impervious areas and nocturnal SUHI can be spotted. The four major SUHI zones from Fig. 3b are precisely overlaid on the first three urban LCZ classes. New Delhi being a planned city, was mostly covered by openly arranged buildings. This led to the fragmentation of SUHI cover at central city areas. The outskirts of the city were mostly covered with bare rock (537.91 km2), soil or sand (589.83 km2). This explained the presence of higher LST values at city surroundings in day time. The distribution of percent land covered by these different LCZ classes in New Delhi region is shown by Fig. 8.

Kolkata city region

The spatial patterns of urban thermal hotspots for the Kolkata city region were validated using the LCZ map shown in Fig. 9. Similar to New Delhi, using the confusion matrix of 500 randomly distributed points over the Kolkata city region, LCZ classification was verified. The overall accuracy achieved was 85.2% and the value of the Kappa statistic was 0.851. The values were highly satisfactory to continue the post classification assessments. Among the urban LCZ classes of Kolkata, compact low rise buildings had maximum areal cover (97.47 km2). This class is visible densely over the central business district of Kolkata city on both sides of river Ganga. The spatial pattern of this class matches perfectly with SUHI locations from Fig. 4a. Openly arranged mid-rise and high-rise buildings can be found at sites marked as C and D in Fig. 9. These locations represent ongoing urbanization and newly developing SUHI over the satellite towns which were marked in Fig. 1b. Apart from LCZ3, large low rise (64.45 km2) and open low rise (62.41 km2) classes also had significant areal extent. The results indicate that the urban micro-climate of the 2nd study region is influenced mostly by low rise buildings than high or mid-rise urban built-up. The very low LST zone from Fig. 4 corresponded to the presence of dense vegetation and low plants from Fig. 9. Out of the 18 classes, agricultural fields or low plants covered the highest land cover (110.98 km2). The area covered by each of the 18 classes is represented by Fig. 10.

Buffer analysis

New Delhi city region

The spatial pattern of urban to non-urban ratio extracted from the 8 buffer zones is shown in Fig. 11. The plot shows that within 20 km from CBD of New Delhi, both dense urban and open urban built-up were prevalent over natural land cover. Clusters of SUHI have developed over this region at night time. Beyond 20 km, the land cover was dominated by natural LCZ classes. The urban to non-urban ratio values were below 1 for both DU/NU and OU/NU.

Kolkata city region

The urban/non-urban ratio for the 12 buffer zones was plotted in Fig. 12. In the 1st circle of 2 km radius, compact urban to non-urban ratio was much greater than 1. The first three buffer rings from the city center mostly contain dense built-up, which led to an intensification of SUHIs. Beyond the 5th buffer or 10 km from the city center, the impact of the natural landscape was greater than the built environment. Both the compact urban to non-urban and open urban to non-urban land cover ratios were less than 1. In the entire transition from center to edge, only two exceptions were observed. One was in the 2nd buffer circle, where the open built-up had lesser extent than the natural LCZ classes. This occurred as a large extent was covered by the river Ganga. Another exception was at the 11th buffer zone, where the open urban cover was greater than a natural land cover. This happened particularly due to the recent development of residential and commercial blocks by converting agricultural fields or filling the wetlands of this region. The presence of high rise buildings (both open and compact) at the city fringes depicted the current nature of urban sprawl.

Contrast in inter-LCZ class LST

New Delhi city region

To investigate the change in LST between all LCZ classes, day time temperature range for each class was extracted. The distribution of LST is shown in Fig. 13. Among the dense and open urban buildings, low-rise built-up resulted in maximum LST rise. The mean LST of LCZ3 was 2.19 °C higher than the mean LST of natural land cover classes, while LCZ6 was 1.36 °C warmer than the natural landscape for the summer season. Another class with notably high LST was LCZ8. This class had the highest observed LST (40.73 °C) within the first 10 LCZ categories. Kwok et al. (2019) had also observed the mean radiant temperature of LCZ8 to be the highest among all classes. A large portion of city fringes were covered with rocky surfaces, bare soil and sand which have very high mean LST. In contrast, water bodies showed the lowest LST (32.2 °C) of all classes, followed by dense vegetation (37.1 °C).

Kolkata city region

The variation between LCZ classes’ LST was explored using maximum LST, mean LST and the minimum LST are plotted in Fig. 14.

The plot shows that the mean LST was highest for LCZ3. The average LST of compact low rise buildings was 3.73 °C higher than the average LST of natural LCZ classes. The mean LST was lowest for the water bodies present, followed by wetlands and then vegetation. In terms of the maximum LST, apart from LCZ3, LCZ8 and LCZ10 were also marked with high temperature (> 38 °C). These classes are particularly causing extreme heating within the city region and the rise of SUHI intensity.

Contribution of LCZ classes in SUHI creation

Based on DI, the percent contribution of LCZ classes in SUHI creation for New Delhi and Kolkata city is shown by the pie-charts in Fig. 15a, b, respectively.

The contrast in results for the two city region was distinct. For the 1st study region, SUHI zones mostly consisted of high-rise and mid-rise buildings. The contribution of LCZ4 was the maximum in SUHI formation (18.18%). Another significant class was heavy industrial areas (LCZ10) that led to high LST zones. In contrast to this, for the 2nd study region LCZ1, LCZ2 and LCZ4 had little contribution in SUHI formation due to their smaller areal cover. LCZ3 was fundamental in creating SUHIs (31.65% contribution). LCZ8 and LCZ10 also had a high percent contribution for the current SUHI pattern. It can be observed that LCZ3 and LCZ10 had a major impact on the urban climate for both study regions. This calls for special attention to the sustainable planning of these classes in any city region.

Validation of key SUHI areas with surface albedo pattern

New Delhi city region

The summer and winter spatial profile of albedo is shown in Fig. 16a, b, respectively. The map of summer 2019 shows low albedo concentrating over the central city region. This validated the high absorption of solar radiation by the urban surface materials. The SUHI location D from Fig. 3b was marked with an albedo of 0.12, while the agricultural fields (LCZD) showed an albedo of 0.27. The SUHI significantly decreased over region A in Fig. 16a from summer to winter. This change can be described with the surface material character and albedo value of this region. The albedo value of this area changed from 0.18 in summer to 0.32 in winter. Within the city, LCZ3 was marked with low albedo as 0.11 (location B). The LCZ5 area (C) located within 4 km of B, displayed a surface albedo of 0.20. The albedo maps suggested the significance of the urban areas with a higher percentage of paved surfaces in the absorption of solar radiation and rise of LST. The low albedo values of streets, roofs and high albedo of cool surfaces were compared with the works of Falasca et al. (2019) and were found to be of a similar range.

Kolkata city region

The surface albedo maps of summer and winter 2019 for the Kolkata region are shown in Fig. 17a, b, respectively. The CBD region from Fig. 1b showed a distinct rise in albedo from summer to winter. This explained the decrease in SUHI extent in winter over Kolkata. The albedo of this zone was marked to be 0.08 in summer. It increased to 0.26 in winter. The low albedo at the southern fringes of the Kolkata city boundary (A) in summer was absent in the winter season. This precisely matched with the LST profile of Fig. 4a, c. The albedo values were again compared with the average albedo of a similar city region dominated by impervious surfaces. The albedo values obtained from the study conducted over an urban patch of Terni city from central Italy (Bonafoni et al. 2017) varied between 0.22 and 0.27 which was similar to the current case studies.

Proposed SUHI mitigation strategies based on LCZs

New Delhi city region

  • In the case of the first study area, LCZ4 had the maximum contribution in SUHI creation. As SUHI zones were dominated by mid-rise and high-rise buildings, green walls, green roofs over commercial offices may help to reduce SUHI intensity. The roof area of LCZ1, LCZ2, LCZ4 and LCZ5 can be converted into low plants (LCZD). LCZD had a remarkably low LST range among all classes (Fig. 13) and hence this can mitigate extreme heat waves of summer. The presence of mid-rise and high-rise buildings can also be applied for better street gorge ventilation (Ghiaus et al. 2006). Construction of new buildings has to be monitored so that the existing street gorge ventilation does not get blocked. New constructions must be aligned in a way to enhance air flow. This can eventually bring down SUHI intensity.

  • The presence of bare ground has resulted in very high day time LST, as shown in Fig. 3a. These regions can be converted into agricultural areas (LCZD) or major recreational zones.

Kolkata city region

  • Figs. 14 and 15b suggest LCZ3 as the most critical urban land use category giving rise to SUHI phenomena. From Fig. 10, it was noted that LCZ3 has the second highest areal cover for the study region. It was also located over the center of Kolkata city (Fig. 9). Hence, reducing the urban to non-urban ratio would be of top priority for these compact low-rise buildings. Due to dense built-up pattern, complete rezoning will be difficult to achieve. Green roofs may also not thrive due to shadow on low-rise buildings. For LCZ3, increasing vegetation along the roads can be suggested. As this class belongs to CBD region, the road density is expected to be high. Another strategy of adjusting albedo can be used over these low-rise dense buildings. Like changing the color of building facades to a high solar reflective surface, can drastically reduce urban LST in summer days (Watkins et al. 2007).

  • A similar assessment can be drawn for other LCZ classes critical to the thermal environment and also has a dense low-rise building pattern. SUHI caused by LCZ8 and LCZ10 can be mitigated by applying strategies likewise.

  • For mid-rise and high-rise buildings (LCZ1, LCZ2, LCZ4 and LCZ5) SUHI intensity can be reduced with green roof, green façade and vertical gardening. A vast roof area of these buildings can be turned into low plants (LCZD), as it had minimum contribution in SUHI formation (0.02% from Fig. 15b). As for this study region, open buildings were present in currently sprawling suburban areas. Hence, maintain street gorge ventilation instead of unplanned urbanization can suppress the growing SUHI problem.

  • At the fringes of the study area, LCZE and LCZF had the greatest contribution in rising day time LST. These bare surfaces can be used for green landscaping and the creation of parks or other recreational zones. Among vegetation classes, dense tree areas were noted with mean LST 0.88 °C less than LST of scattered tree regions (Fig. 14). This signifies the cooling effect produced by parks, recreational zones and forests consisting of dense vegetation. Protection of the identified patches of LCZA should be of top priority.

Conclusion

A contrast in the day time SUHI pattern was distinctly observed for the two tropical mega cities, namely New Delhi and Kolkata. The severe growth of SUHI was hence addressed through the nocturnal LST pattern in the cities. The night time SUHIs of summer season (May, 2019) were found to cover 13.99% of the area of New Delhi city region and 22.26% of the area of the Kolkata city region. In terms of seasonal variations, the urban thermal characteristics were similar for both cities. The SUHI intensity in summer night was 2.89 °C higher and the areal extent was 18.85% greater than that of the winter night for New Delhi. In the case of Kolkata, summer SUHI intensity was 1.35 °C greater and SUHI cover was 29.10% higher than the winter scenario. Comparing the two megacities, it was observed that the maximum SUHI intensity of New Delhi was 3.35 °C higher than Kolkata. The difference in SUHI intensity was expected to be due to the population size difference between the two cities. The outdoor thermal comfort index depicted significant heat stress was observed over the central city areas, during both day and night time. Even after sunset, in summer of 2019, moderate heat stress was witnessed over 14.13% area of the New Delhi region. In contrast, areas with moderate heat stress covered 68.23% of the Kolkata region in the summer night of the year 2019. The poorly planned CBD region of Kolkata was the major cause behind the high concentration of SUHI. The lack of space and greenery at center Kolkata was prominent as the compact urban to non-urban ratio was above 15 for the first buffer ring; for New Delhi this ratio was below 6 for the first buffer ring from center. Also, tropical climate with more humidity had greater heat stress at night time than the city at continental location.

The results identified LCZ3, LCZ8 and LCZ10 to critically affect the thermal environment, while LCZ9 of the built classes prominently reduced SUHI effects. LCZ and corresponding LST range were linked through different approaches. The approach using distribution index gave a better understanding than inter-LCZ LST computation. Albedo mapping also brought out the importance of surface material. The dense built-up and paved surfaces of LCZ3 resulted in an albedo value as low as 0.80. The average LST of this class was 3.73 °C higher than the average LST of natural LCZ classes. In Kolkata, 31% of the total summer SUHI was caused by surface characteristics of LCZ3 class and nearly 10% of the summer SUHI of New Delhi was due to the same climate zone. Urgent sustainable strategies have to be applied to prevent further development of SUHI over satellite towns. Consideration of suburban areas in the study brought out the poor thermal environment situations and newly developing SUHIs beyond city boundaries. The residences in the outskirts were mostly comprised of high-rise buildings. These built-up types are suitable for sustainable city planning as vast roof area of these buildings can be turned into low plants (LCZD). LCZD has the lowest contribution (0.02%) in SUHI formation. The high-rise buildings in newly developing outskirts also aid in gorge ventilation. For central city regions, where altering built-up density is difficult, applying high albedo surfaces may bring down the SUHI intensity. With a rise of 0.10 in albedo value, up to 2.48 °C fall in LST was noted over New Delhi city. The applied methodology helped us to successfully characterize critical LCZs for different cities with varied built-up morphology. Accordingly, mitigation strategies were suggested for improved urban health and ecological stability.
