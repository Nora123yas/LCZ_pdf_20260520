Local climate zone classification with different source data in Xi’an,China

Local climate zone classification with different source data in Xi’an,China

, , [...], and
Volume 28, Issue 9

Abstract

The city’s local climate zone (LCZ) has been considered as a useful guiding tool for urban climate mapping and classification of urban canopy in cities. However, the LCZ classification methods and results are different in different cities in different countries and regions. Therefore, it is necessary to conduct a targeted research that would take into account local urban morphology and canopy characteristics. In the current work, a LCZ classification method is proposed. This would provide a useful guide for city planning and operation. In the newly proposed method, the LCZ types were first adjusted, according to the characteristics and urban morphology of Chinese cities, in relation to existence of high- and low-rise buildings and high density residential population and land cover of greeneries. Based on investigations of two LCZ classifications of the City of Xi’an, a coupling method was proposed. This method couples the World Urban Database and Access Portal Tools method based on remote sensing data with the classification method based on road network divided by land parcels. The proposed method was validated in this study against the actual land use and construction data that were surveyed in Xi’an, which showed acceptable accuracy. Our results have illustrated the feasibility of our proposed classification for urban LCZ mapping in China.

Introduction

Urbanization has brought a great influence on the local climate and environment in urban areas. A continuous series of environmental problems are occurring in urban areas, such as air pollution and urban heat island; these are increasingly affecting people’s daily life. By the end of 2015, 56.1% of the total population of China reside in cities, 1 which bring a great pressure to the city’s environmental problems. Long term and wide range of haze weather which could directly affect the health of residents are receiving unprecedented attention in China. Sustainable urban planning is a considerable way to alleviate the urban environmental problems, taking into account energy consumption, transportation, local economic developments, human interaction and requirements, usage of resources, parks, recreation and greeneries. 2 However, sustainable urban planning can be affected by many factors, such as availability of technologies, existence of industries and availability of commerce services and education provisions. The application of latest research technologies and achievements on urban climatology in urban planning is still very limited in China at the present.

In traditional studies on urban climate, the main focus has been to compare the differences between urban and rural areas. Due to the lack of systematic and universal theories and methods, people’s cognition has been influenced by the two elemental concepts of ‘urban–rural’. 3 However, with the development of urbanization, the traditional methods encountered many deficiencies, mainly reflected in the lacking of standardization of surface structure and surface coverage of urban canopy and rural areas that used to describe artificial sources and divisions, and the lacking of reported observations and analyses to provide strict protocol for such studies. 4

Stewart and Oke 5 proposed a concept of local climate zone (LCZ), with an aim to resolve problems caused by urban–rural classification. The LCZ method is known as the international standard method to characterize the influence of ground cover and heat island. 6 This standard method has been widely studied and used by many researchers.7–13 LCZs are defined as regions of uniform surface cover, considering urban structures, materials and human activities that span hundreds of metres to several kilometres in horizontal scale.5–7 In general, each type of LCZ has similar characteristics of climatological conditions, such as temperature, wind and pollutant concentrations. 9 The greatest advantage of LCZ is to highlight the influence of different surface forms on the regional climate formation in urban areas, especially inside the urban canopy layer, which are closely related to the life of residents. The method of dividing the urban canopy layer by LCZ provides a reference standard for the description of urban internal parameters and also provides a feasible method for the study of urban climate and environment.

The division of LCZs can be based on geographic information system (GIS) and remote sensing image data, 9 or detailed urban planning data and on-site observations.8,14 In the horizontal space division, it can be divided into square grids of fixed precision (100–200 m).7,9 The horizontal space can also be divided by road network into parcels.8,15 The former is relatively direct, while the latter is more likely to be accepted by planners, because blocks are usually the basic control units in urban planning. 15

When the division method based on GIS and remote sensing image data introduced by Bechtel et al. 9 was used in China, the accuracy was found to be much lower than when it was used in European and/or American cities.6–8 The reason could be due to variations in the height and density of buildings in a large range of different Chinese cities. 8 Therefore, it is necessary to discuss the division method of LCZ in detail.

In the current work, the concept and method of LCZ division was studied, taking Xi’an as an example. This study focused on the main city areas within the third ring road in the City of Xi’an. In the current work, not only the source of data was compared, but also the characteristics of application in urban planning and the classification methods were investigated.

LCZ definition and division method

Modification of the LCZ classification standard

The LCZ is an index system developed for evaluation of urban heat island. 5 Each type of LCZ has similar characteristics, such as land cover, surface structure, material and human activities. These characteristics of each LCZ can be classified by a standard set of parameters, including sky view factor, aspect ratio, impervious surface fraction, pervious surface fraction and height of roughness elements. The index system of LCZ could generalize most characteristics of a city but does not include cultural features. 9

The LCZ classification standard and the division method introduced by Bechtel et al. 9 were developed based on studies in European cities, where the urban morphology and canopy structure are significantly different from those in China. In Chinese cities, there are more high-rise buildings with a higher density of resident population. There are distinctive variations in building heights in various cities. Therefore, when classifying LCZ in Chinese cities, the traditional method was found inadequate, and this should include a consideration of Chinese characteristics of urban morphology in various cities in various regions of China. 8

Buildings in Chinese cities can be divided into four categories: low-rise buildings (three storeys or below), multi-storey buildings (or mid-low-rise buildings, 4–6 storeys), mid-high-rise buildings (7–15 storeys) and high-rise buildings (16 storeys or more). Taking Xi’an City as an example, high-rise buildings have been built rapidly in the past several years. In these years, most of the newly built residential buildings are super high-rise buildings, of approximately 33 storeys (about 100 m). The existing LCZ classification standard5,9 is difficult to distinguish between the tall high-rise buildings and low-high-rise buildings, thus it cannot reflect the height variation characteristics of buildings in Chinese cities.

In our current study, according to building height variation characteristics in Chinese cities, the LCZ classification standard proposed by Stewart et al., 5 Ren et al. 6 and Bechtel et al. 9 was modified by subdividing the LCZ types ‘LCZ 2’ and ‘LCZ 5’ for classification of climate zones in a city, 16 as shown in Table 1. In our classification system, the building heights of LCZ types in the ‘LCZ 2’ and ‘LCZ 5’ are further subdivided into two types: ‘LCZ 2(i)’ which is defined as a land parcel with ‘compact mid-high-rise’ buildings and ‘LCZ 5(i)’ is defined as a land parcel with ‘open mid-high-rise’ buildings, where buildings are ‘mid-high-rise buildings’ with a height of 7–15 storeys; ‘LCZ 2(ii)’ is defined as a land parcel with ‘compact mid-low-rise’ buildings and ‘LCZ 5(ii)’ is defined as a land parcel with ‘open mid-low-rise’ buildings, where ‘multi-storey buildings’ with height of 4–6 storeys are mainly present. In this current study, buildings of 16 or more storeys are defined as ‘high-rise’ buildings, so as to include the emergence of large numbers of super high-rise buildings of around 33 storeys or more in many cities in China; in contrast to the LCZ classification standard proposed by Stewart and Oke, 5 in which ‘high-rise’ buildings were defined as buildings of 10 or more storeys.

Division method of LCZ

The initial data for LCZ division could be obtained from field investigation, which is too laborious. Generally, the initial data for LCZ division could be obtained from aerial photos, land cover or land use maps, satellite images (such as Google earth (GE)) and urban planning data.

Based on detailed urban planning data, the method of decision trees to classify LCZ was applied in Brno, 12 Szeged 17 and Hong Kong. 18 However, obtaining detailed and high-resolution urban information is often difficult. Therefore, remote sensing image is widely accepted as suspicious by many researchers, as advantages in supervised classification remained unproven. However, there are some difficulties in dividing LCZs when using remote sensing images. For example, when defining the LCZ classification standard, there has been a concern regarding the climatology characteristics of the urban canopy layer, while ignoring the local architectural cultural details, such as building materials and vegetation type, leading to inconsistencies in the classification; for example, one LCZ type may have very different spectral characteristic in different cities. To solve these problems, a method called World Urban Database and Access Portal Tools (WUDAPT) has been introduced, which is a classification method that combines remote sensing image and mapping by GE. 9 The WUDAPT method has the advantage of acquisitioning data that might be inconvenient in general sense to obtain and also would provide a fast data processing, high accuracy and objectivity. An idealized LCZ partition process can be divided into three steps by WUDAPT. 9

Pre-processing using the Landsat data: A certain study area is generally determined by clipping the selected Landsat images with the administrative area or some other characteristic boundary lines of the city, so as to reduce the computational tasks. This pre-processed image is re-sampled in a resolution of 100–150 m, in order to get the spectral signal that represents the local-scale urban structures (e.g. buildings in the block), rather than a single smaller object (e.g. one or part of a building).

Training selection sampling: Representative blocks are selected by polygons as training samples of each LCZ class, which is then saved as a ‘**.kml’ data.

LCZ classification using the software ‘SAGA-GIS’ 19 : The pre-processed Landsat images and the selected training sampled data (the ‘**.kml’ data) are imported into the software SAGA-GIS. Using the SAGA-GIS, the LCZ classification in the study area is calculated and conducted with a random forest classifier by comparing the similarity between the training samples and the blocks in the study area.

The WUDAPT method has been illustrated to produce high accuracy in the classification of some European cities, 9 except for the interface of two LCZ grids. 20 The morphology of urban canopy in China, take for an example, Xi’an, can be characterized as consisting of tall, high-rise buildings with high density in distribution and significant diverse variations in height.

In the current work, the morphology characteristics of an urban canopy layer in Xi’an, China, was considered. Different LCZ division methods were introduced and performed for the classification of LCZ in the city of Xi’an to test the accuracy of methods, so the validated method can be used in Chinese cities.

Mapping of LCZ in Xi’an

Xi’an is located in the middle of Guanzhong Plain. The Weihe River flows through the north of the city and the Qinling Mountains are situated in the south. The city has a temperate monsoon climate, with a hot and rainy summer, cold and less rain in winter. The average annual wind speed is about 1.6 m/s, with a prevailing north-easterly wind. The annual average temperature is 13–13.7°C. The coldest monthly average temperature is −1.2°C to 0°C in January and the hottest monthly average temperature is 26.3–26.6°C in July. The current study focused on the main city area within the main beltway, with a total area of about 460 km2.

LCZ division based on road network blocks and urban planning data

Urban road network divides the city into lots of block units, which are the basic control targets in urban planning. Generally, there is little change in the underlying surface structure in each plot unit, resulting in the local microclimate characteristics. Therefore, it is reasonable to divide the local climate areas according to plot units. The urban road network divides the main area of Xi’an city into about 2800 blocks. According to the planned building height and density, and greening ratio data in each block, artificial judgements were carried out in all the blocks and the LCZ map was proposed, as shown in Figure 1. The planning data were collected from the city’s planning department and electric maps (https://map.baidu.com/).

LCZ map of Xi’an (divided with planning data).

As shown in Figure 1, building types are mainly LCZ 2(ii) in the downtown areas of Xi’an, which are mainly compact multi-storey buildings. Around the downtown areas, there are increasing presence of building types of LCZ 1 and LCZ 4, indicating the increase of high-rise buildings around the city. Thus, the overall morphology of the urban canopy layer was a ‘bowl’ shape, which is not conducive to urban ventilation. The compact low-rise buildings (LCZ 3) are mainly distributed outside the second ring road, in the rural–urban fringe zone, where there are many villages. There are a large number of industrial buildings (LCZ 8) in the north-west and northern areas of Xi’an city, while farmland (LCZ D) and/or woodland (LCZ B) are distributed in the north-east and south-west suburbs.

In Figure 1, the LCZ map divides the urban area of Xi’an based on road networks. The urban planning data show the actual land use and surface coverage conditions in the study areas. In fact, division method of landscape by road network is a regularly used method in urban planning and design. However, artificial classification of LCZ types is time consuming. Also, certain climatological knowledge is required to classify LCZ by division of landscape, thus the results were greatly influenced by human factors. When the urban planning data were incomplete or difficult to obtain, field survey is needed, which is more difficult to achieve.

There are many advantages of the WUDAPT method that was based on remote sensing image. The process is automatic and saves labour investment. The remote sensing images can be resource sharing. However, when the height variation of buildings is remarkable, there are too many strange alterations of LZC between adjacent square grids, thus the entire map seems to have a lot of small spots. There is no clear landmark as well, thus it is difficult to meet the needs of convenient applications. Therefore, it is necessary to carry out effective post-processing.

Discussion and analysis

The impact of urban construction characteristics on LCZ classification in China

The above two methods about LCZ classification are obviously different. The method of LCZ classification based on road network and planning data can reflect the actual land use and surface coverage characteristics perfectly, with high accuracy and acceptability by city planners. However, the human operation task would be large and could be affected by operators. The WUDAPT method of LCZ classification that was based on remote sensing image data is highly automated for urban LCZ mapping, with less labour investment. The remote sensing images could be resource sharing. However, when the height and density of the building vary greatly, this method is inconvenient.

Great variations in height and density of buildings are the basic features of urbanization in China leading to lots of small spots in appearance in the LCZ map. Therefore, the above methods should be improved. A method coupling the computer software and road network divided parcels was introduced in our current study based on the WUDAPT method. In our method, the LCZ type of all pixel units within each parcel was judged and the most frequently appeared LCZ type was generally chosen as the LCZ type of parcels. The final division results of urban LCZs were displayed at a clear resolution showing land parcels divided by road network. The combination of remote sensing image classification and road network parcel unit can be realized by the partition statistics operation under the ‘Spatial Analyst’ in ArcGIS, in which the statistical type selection, ‘Majority’, was used in processing. The processed LCZ map with the currently proposed method is shown in Figure 4. This method realized the division of LCZ in cities automatically, which reduced the influence of human input and human factors. Also, the current proposed method reduced the small spots in the whole LCZ map. Generally, the LCZ map as shown in Figure 4 is more easy to be accepted and operated by urban planners.

LCZ division results by coupling of remote sensing image data and road network.

Analysis of the accuracy and influencing factors of LCZ division results

The urban LCZ mapping coupled with the remote sensing image data with the road network utilizes the advantages of the two previous LCZ mapping methods as discussed in the previous sections of this paper.

The confusion matrix analysis was conducted by comparing with the LCZ mapping results based on urban planning and survey data, as shown in Table 4. The overall accuracy of the currently proposed method is 0.52. It should be emphasized that, in the current work, only the main urban area was considered, with a large suburban out of scope areas, where the land surface was uniform and the accuracy of LCZ division was high. The overall accuracy of the method is acceptable. Therefore, the LCZ mapping method based on the remote sensing image data and the road network divided parcels is adequate and acceptable for LCZ classification in Chinese cities.

Table 4 shows that the main error affecting the overall accuracy was generally produced between similar LCZs, such as between LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 (ii), between LCZ 1 and LCZ 4, and between LCZ B and LCZ D. Among them, LCZ 4 and LCZ 5 (i) were of low accuracy, as presented in Table 4. This was mainly due to the land satellite data, which do not have elevation data, but only pass multispectral bands of images. For example, with the thermal infrared band of images, it is difficult to distinguish between the small high-rise and high-rise buildings. On the other hand, due to a lack of elevation data, trees on both sides of the roads are of similar spectral characteristics with some constructed areas; therefore, leading to frequent misclassification. Loss sample number can lead to low accuracy as well, such as LCZ E and LCZ F, as shown in Table 4.

In Xi’an city, the LCZ E was mainly accounted for with the presence of plazas and parking areas, which generally appeared accompanying with other LCZ types. Thus, the LCZ E was difficult to be identified. For example, the south plaza of Daming Palace Relics Park in Xi’an should be classified as LCZ E, while in fact, the entire Daming Palace Relics Park was classified as LCZ D. Affected by the resampling method and resolution, errors were often found at the road boundaries, where multiple types were mixed together in resampling processes. LCZ 2(ii) is the most confusing LCZ type, which frequently appeared accompanying with other types. The introduction of combined LCZ types in one parcel can effectively reduce such kind of errors. In downtown areas, where the road network is generally very dense, some of the land parcels that are too small to meet the requirements for LCZ classification should be modified in subsequent research applications.

Conclusion

In the current work, the standard of LCZ was first introduced and improved to suit the characteristics of urbanization in China. Two methods of LCZ classification were investigated for urban mapping of the city of Xi’an.

The method of LCZ classification based on road network and planning data was found to illustrate the actual land use and surface coverage characteristics perfectly, with a high accuracy and would be acceptable for use by city planners. However, the human operation task was large and could be affected by operators. The WUDAPT method was highly automated, with less labour investment and resource sharing. However, when the height and density of the building vary greatly, this method would become inadequate. Thus, a method coupling the WUDAPT method with the road network divided land parcels was introduced for our study.

Confusion matrix analysis on the coupling method showed a reasonable accuracy. Further investigation revealed that the resampling method, training sample size and regional variations were the main factors affecting the LCZ classification results. The combination of LCZ types in one parcel could be effective to reduce errors in the LCZ classification.
